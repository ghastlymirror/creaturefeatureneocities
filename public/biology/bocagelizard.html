Bocage's Wall Lizard

  • Stats:

  • Observation Date: 14/06/2020
  • Species: Podarcis bocagei
  • Family: Lacertidae (True Lizards)
  • Order: Squamata
  • Class: Reptilia
  • Origin: Iberian Peninsula
  • Habitat: Temperate Forests, gardens, urban areas, shrubland, sand dunes

Characteristics

This small species of lizard is endemic to Northwestern of the Iberian Peninsula and to this region alone. It has a very robust and flat body, and adults can measure up to 7cm from head to toe (not counting its lengthy tail).

It has a sligthy round head, with small eyes. The males are a lot colorful and larger compared to females, with a green color (lighter green in the lateral dorsal areas with promients dark markings), while females have a brown color, with some well defined green or yellow lines.

They also possess white flanks, althought they change to a more intense yellow or orange color during mating season with the exception of the females who have the same appearance all year long.

Due to its appearance, this animal can easily be mistaken with another lizard species, the Carbonell´s wall Lizard. One way to tell it appart, to check for blue spots on the edge of the belly, if these are absent then it´s probably a Bocage, its also larger too.

Diet consists mostly of spiders and insects, specifically beetles. However, they themselves are preyed upon by snakes, other lizards and some birds like the Common Kestrel (Falco tinnunculus), and the Ocellated Lizard (Timon lepidus)

One interesting way they have to flee predation is by loosing their tail, which can be regrown, so its not unsual to find some lizards missing one.

Between November and February, they enter hibernation under rocks, however some individuals have be found to be active during the entire year if the temperature doesn´t get to below 10ºC.

Meanwhile, mating season occurs during from March till June, they usually lay clutches of 2-9 eggs. It takes up between two to three months for them to hatch. Juveniles look a bit similar to adult females with a green tail.

They only reach sexual maturity in their second year of life, with their lifespan lasting up to four years.

Fortunately, this a very common species and is under the "least concern" Conservation status due to having a very stable population.

One fun fact, it was named after the Portuguese Zoologist José Vicente Barbosa du Bocage. who is actually a relative to a famous Portuguese poet with the same name.
