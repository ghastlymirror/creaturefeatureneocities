White Wagtail

  • Stats:

  • Observation Date: 30/03/2019
  • Species: Motacilla alba (subspecies: M. a. alba)
  • Family: Motacillidae
  • Order: Passeriformes
  • Class: Aves
  • Origin: Europe from the Iberian Peninsula to Ural Mountains, Turkey, the Levant, Iceland, the Faroe Islands and Greenland. Occasionally found on Britain, South Europe and Africa
  • Habitat: Open sunny areas with low vegetation and near water, Urban and Rural areas

Characteristics

The white wagtail got its name due to the way it moves its tail. It has a grey, white and black color, with the pattern varying from subspecies to subspecies and measure from 16 to 19cm in lenght. Females can have a lighter coloring compared to the males, and juveniles are mostly grey Its has been documented to exist at least 11 subspecies that can be found in Europe, Asia, North Africa.

They prefer to live in open areas avoiding dry and densely forested areas. In fact, they seem to have adapted very well to urban areas, and have built nests in human structures or stones crevices. Its also not uncommon to see them in parking lots foraging for food.

White wagtails prey on insects and other invetrebrates (beetles, flies, snails, maggots, dragonflies, spiders, worms.) and maybe even feed off aquatic animals like small fish and crustaceans when near the water. Depending on the climate, their diets might all include seeds and plants.

Mating season occurs between April and August, building their nests hidden under vegetation, small crevices or holes and even old nests Males are the first ones to start construction while the females finish the rest.

They can lay 3-8 eggs, these can be cream-coloured, with  bluish-green or turquoise tint and heavily spotted with reddish-brown, which are incubated by both parents. The eggs will then hatch between 11-16 days, chicks will fledge at 16 days old, but the parents will still feed them at least for a bit longer even after they leave the nest.

Breeding pairs are monogomous and territorial specially during mating season and will often chase away other birds.

One interesting thing to note, is that there has been some speculation why these birds keep wagging their tail, some say its to signal submission to other wagtails, others to help flush out prey with their movements, Althought the most acceptable theory is to signal to predators that its in an alert state to discoourage them from pursuit.
